Overview

Problem & Objective

Additive manufacturing — particularly FDM, SLS and SLM — produces complex parts whose surface integrity directly affects functional performance. Evaluating microstructural quality requires stable, repeatable, motorised positioning of a digital microscope across the specimen surface. Manual adjustment introduces operator error and makes systematic scanning impossible.

The objective of this thesis was to design, build and validate a fully custom 3-axis CNC inspection stand capable of automating the scanning process, enabling future implementation of micro-image stitching to generate high-resolution composite surface maps from sequential microscope captures.

The entire system — mechanical structure, motion hardware, electronics and firmware — was engineered from scratch using first-principles calculations, then fabricated and assembled in-house using laser cutting, TIG welding, 3D printing and manual machining.

Mechanical Design

3-Axis Motion Architecture

X
Trapezoidal Lead Screw
Ø8 mm, 2 mm pitch · Two Ø8 mm guide rails · 4× SC8UU linear bearings · NEMA 17 stepper
Y
GT2 Toothed Belt
MGN15C linear rail · 20-tooth pulley · Belt tensioner with adjustable screw · NEMA 17 stepper
Z
C-Beam Gantry
4080 aluminium extrusion · Anti-backlash T8 nut · V-slot wheels · NEMA 17 stepper — carries the microscope

The structure is built on a 3 mm laser-cut steel chassis (DD11C020DH), TIG-welded at critical junctions and painted RAL 9005. Guide rail parallelism on the X-axis was enforced using custom 3D-printed jigs to eliminate lateral deviation during assembly. Coupling between motors and screws uses bellows-type flexible shaft couplings to absorb misalignment without transmitting bending moments.

A required motor torque calculation was performed for the worst-case axis (X, carrying 4.5 kg). With a linear bearing friction coefficient of 0.01, the minimum torque required was 0.146 Nm — well within the NEMA 17 HS4401S rated holding torque of 0.40 Nm.

Fabrication Process

Build Sequence

01
CAD Design — Fusion 360
Full 3D parametric model of the stand designed in Fusion 360. All steel parts exported as DXF for laser cutting. 3D-printed parts sliced in PrusaSlicer with dual-colour first layer for visual assembly guidance.
02
Laser Cutting — TRUMPF TruLaser 2030 fibre
DD11C020DH steel cut to DXF drawings — 2 mm and 3 mm thicknesses. Parts cleaned with acetone, then drilled, countersunk and tapped for M3–M6 fasteners using a Bosch PBD 40 bench drill at 1200 rpm.
03
TIG Welding & Surface Finishing
Z-axis arm assembly welded from three interlocked steel pieces using notch-and-fit alignment. Post-weld: degreased, primed (Bostik), then painted RAL 9005 matt black for corrosion protection.
04
3D Printing & Custom Fixtures
All non-structural parts printed in PLA+ on Sovol SV06 Plus. Included: motor mounts, cable chain brackets, electronics enclosure with dual-filament colour markings, X-axis parallelism jigs cut from plexiglass on an 80W CO₂ laser.
05
Electronics Assembly & Wiring
Arduino UNO + CNC Shield V3 + 3× DRV8825 drivers wired to the stepper motors. 14.8 V Li-Po distributes power via bus bar to DRV8825s, two LM2596 step-down modules (5 V fan, 12 V LED strip) and the main switch.
06
Firmware & Control
GRBL firmware flashed to Arduino. Steps/mm calibrated per axis. UGS (Universal G-code Sender) used as the PC interface. PS5 controller mapped via DS4Windows for manual jogging. Homing sequence configured on all 6 endstops (X±, Y±, Z±).

The completed stand successfully automates microscope positioning across specimen surfaces. The modular architecture supports future integration of micro-image stitching — capturing sequential overlapping frames and compositing them into a single high-resolution surface map. This would transform the stand into a fully autonomous surface characterisation instrument suitable for QC in additive manufacturing workflows.
